# Commercial Licensing

## TL;DR

The project is licensed under [AGPL-3.0-or-later](LICENSE.md). If your organization can comply with the AGPL, you do not need a commercial license — small companies, individuals incorporated for tax reasons, freelancers, and anyone running unmodified copies are entirely welcome to use the project without ever talking to me.

If your use case is incompatible with the AGPL — for example, embedding the code in a proprietary product, or running it as a hosted service whose modifications you can't release — a commercial license is available and is negotiated bespoke. There is no published price list; pricing and terms are scoped per inquiry.

## Why two licenses exist

Open source is a generosity that is regularly weaponized against the people who write it. The pattern is well-documented: a large company picks up an open-source project, builds a service around it, contributes nothing back, and captures most of the value the original maintainers and contributors produced. Redis, Elastic, MongoDB, Sentry, and others moved away from traditional open-source licensing because of exactly that dynamic.

The dual-licensing setup here exists to prevent that pattern. AGPL is the default specifically because it is the strongest copyleft license available; corporate legal teams that won't tolerate AGPL obligations have the option to pay for a commercial license instead of free-riding. **The intent is to stop predatory corporate behavior, not to engage in it.** I am not trying to extract money from individual users, hobbyists, freelancers, small operators, or contributors.

## Where commercial revenue goes (intent, not contract)

This section describes intent. It is deliberately not a binding commitment of specific amounts, percentages, or timing. Specific mechanics will depend on what revenue (if any) materializes and what tax/legal structure is appropriate at the time. I would rather under-promise and over-deliver than make a precise commitment I might fail to keep.

If commercial licensing produces meaningful revenue:

- **A substantial portion of net revenue is intended to go back to the open-source ecosystem.** Typically via funding other projects through GitHub Sponsors, Open Collective, or direct grants — especially to maintainers and projects this work depends on or builds on top of. Returning value to upstream is the default disposition for any net revenue beyond what is needed to sustain my own continued work on the project.
- **Significant contributors will be recognized in a way that reflects the proportion and impact of their work.** This is intentionally vague: I am not going to make a specific dollar promise to a specific contributor in this document, and the reason is not stinginess — it is that promising specific compensation creates an employment-or-contractor classification problem I am trying to avoid, and worse, it sets up the trap where someone invests substantial work expecting payment that turns out to be a token or nothing. If you are a contributor and meaningful revenue arrives, expect me to reach out.
- **Some portion stays with me** to fund continued work on the project. Neither "all of it" nor "none of it"; the open-source giveback above is intended to be the larger share.

What this is **not**:

- A profit-sharing agreement.
- An employment offer or contractor relationship.
- A binding commitment of any specific amount, percentage, or timing.
- An invitation to contribute work in expectation of payment.

The [Contributor License Agreement](CLA.md) is what it says it is: a copyright and patent grant that lets me dual-license. It is not consideration for compensation, and contributing should be done because you want to, not because you expect a payout. The intent statement above is what I expect to do *if* the project earns; it does not change the legal nature of contributions.
